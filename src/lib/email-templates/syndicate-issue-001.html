Issue #001
Your comp plan doesn't need a redesign. It needs a bake cycle.

If you rewrite the comp plan every year before the last one has been paid out cleanly, you're not "staying agile."

You're skipping the bake cycle. And it's costing you more than you think.

In Alexander Group's 2024 Sales Compensation Trends Survey, only 21% of companies rated their 2023 plans "very effective" — but 91% planned to make changes anyway. That's not optimization. That's churn.

The Take

You're redesigning before you've diagnosed.

Every January: Sales says last year's plan "didn't work," Finance rewrites rates, Sales Ops rebuilds the calculator, and by March everyone pretends the new plan was always the plan.

Here's the failure: you never diagnosed what the old plan actually caused. You heard noise — one loud region, a few angry reps, a CFO who didn't like the payout curve — and called it "feedback."

That's not a redesign. That's a reflex.

Use This — Exec Litmus Test

If you can't answer "What behavior did the plan create?" with evidence, you're not ready to redesign. You're ready to diagnose.

The Idea

Your plan needs a Bake Loop.

Bake Loop: launch → observe → measure → patch → then redesign.

01Deployment Reality — first 30 days
  • Data settles. Crediting rules hit edge cases. Territories overlap.
  • You discover the "field you're paying on" is unreliable.
Use This — Fast Diagnostic

List the 5 fields that drive pay. If any are <90% reliable, fix data before redesign.

02Governance Signal — exceptions repeat

Every exception is a plan confession. If you're approving the same override over and over, it's a structural gap.

Use This — One Rule

No exception closes without: root cause + fix target + owner + due date.

03Behavior Signal — patterns show up
Use This — Three Checks

Deal timing: if bookings cluster at period-end, you built a threshold game.

Discounting: compare discount rates for reps near 95–105% vs everyone else.

Reclassification: if "new logo" spikes without pipeline growth, definitions are being exploited.
